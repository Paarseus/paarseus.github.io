Abstract

The Legged Robotics Lab is a 501(c)(3) nonprofit research organization founded at Cal Poly Pomona to give students hands-on experience building and programming legged robots. With 30+ active members and $10,000+ in secured funding, the lab develops quadruped and bipedal robotic platforms while providing curriculum and workshops covering embedded systems, control theory, and robot kinematics. Current work includes a ros2_control hardware interface for a 5-DOF Robstride arm using the MIT mini-cheetah CAN motor protocol.

Organization & Mission

Founded at Cal Poly Pomona as a registered 501(c)(3) nonprofit, the Legged Robotics Lab provides undergraduate students with hands-on robotics research experience typically available only to graduate students. The organization secured $10,000+ in funding through donor outreach, corporate sponsorships, and grant proposals. As founder and president, I manage the lab's technical direction, develop curriculum, and coordinate 30+ active members across multiple project teams.

ros2_control Hardware Interface

The lab's current flagship project is a ros2_control hardware interface plugin for a 5-DOF Robstride robotic arm. The interface implements the MIT mini-cheetah CAN motor protocol for high-bandwidth, low-latency motor control. The system uses lifecycle management for safe startup and shutdown sequences, with 50 Hz deterministic control loops for position, velocity, and torque command modes.

Quadruped Platform

The quadruped platform serves as the lab's primary research testbed for legged locomotion. Students implement inverse kinematics solvers, gait generators, and balance controllers while gaining hands-on experience with embedded systems, motor drivers, and real-time control. The platform uses the same CAN motor protocol as the robotic arm, enabling shared tooling and cross-project learning.

Workshops & Curriculum

The lab develops and delivers technical workshops covering embedded systems fundamentals, control theory, robot kinematics, and ROS2 development. The curriculum is designed to bring students from zero robotics experience to contributing on research projects within one academic quarter. Workshop materials are open-source and available for other university robotics organizations to adapt.
