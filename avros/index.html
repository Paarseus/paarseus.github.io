End-to-End Autonomous Vehicle Research Platform

Parsa Ghasemi1, Behnam Bahr1, Viviane Seyranian1
1Cal Poly Pomona
ASEE Zone IV Conference, 2026
Paper AVROS Control WebUI PCB

A full-scale autonomous vehicle built for under $15K—giving undergraduates hands-on experience with every layer of the autonomy stack.

Abstract

We present a full-scale autonomous vehicle platform that converts a commodity UTV into a research testbed for $12K–15K—an order of magnitude cheaper than commercial alternatives ($200K+). The platform gives ~30 undergraduates hands-on experience building and debugging every layer of the autonomy stack: LiDAR-based occupancy grids, RTK-GPS/IMU sensor fusion at centimeter-level accuracy, ROS2 Nav2 path planning, distributed CAN bus control across 4 microcontrollers, and a custom PCB for power and sensing.

Validated over 50+ hours of field testing with zero safety incidents, the system has produced 3 conference papers and serves as a replicable model for university AV programs. The entire platform—19,000+ lines of code across navigation, embedded control, teleoperation, and PCB design—is open-source.

System Architecture

The platform is organized into four layers. A perception layer fuses data from a Velodyne VLP-16 LiDAR, Intel RealSense RGB-D camera, and Xsens MTi-680G IMU into a unified sensor abstraction. A planning layer built on ROS2 Nav2 with SmacPlannerHybrid computes kinematically feasible paths, augmented by OSMnx/Dijkstra routing on real OpenStreetMap road networks. A control layer distributes steering, throttle, and brake commands across 4 Teensy 4.1 MCUs over CAN bus at 250 kbps with 20 Hz deterministic loops. A teleoperation layer provides simchair, phone WebUI, and voice control fallback through WebSocket and WebRTC.

Navigation Stack

The navigation system achieves centimeter-level positioning through a GPS/RTK/IMU fusion pipeline with EKF and graceful degradation. A probabilistic occupancy grid achieves 0.2m resolution over 400m² coverage using Bayesian log-odds and Bresenham raycasting. Path following uses a Dynamic Window Approach (DWA) with kinematic bicycle model and Pure Pursuit controller with adaptive speed-dependent lookahead. YOLOPv2 provides drivable area segmentation and lane detection on RealSense RGB-D frames.

Safety Architecture

A four-layer E-STOP architecture with <200ms emergency response and a 500ms hardware watchdog have maintained zero safety incidents across 50+ hours of autonomous operation. The system implements automatic failsafe across the distributed CAN network—if any node loses communication, the vehicle stops.

Cost & Replicability

The complete platform costs $12K–15K, compared to $200K+ for commercial autonomous vehicle research systems. The drive-by-wire architecture, custom PCB designs, and software stack are fully documented and open-source, enabling other universities to replicate the platform for undergraduate education and research.
