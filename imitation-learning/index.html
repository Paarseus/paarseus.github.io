Simplified Multimodal Imitation Learning for Day–Night Autonomous Driving

Aspen Knox1, Parsa Ghasemi1
1Cal Poly Pomona
SCAIR, 2025
Paper

Fusing RGB, thermal, and vehicle telemetry into an imitation learning model that drives itself day or night with only 3 manual interventions.

Abstract

We present a multimodal imitation learning approach that fuses RGB camera (30 Hz), thermal camera (30 Hz), and CAN bus telemetry (20 Hz) to enable autonomous driving across varying lighting conditions. Trained on 50,000+ synchronized frames collected on a campus route, the model learns to generalize driving behavior from day to night without explicit domain adaptation.

The system achieves autonomous navigation with only 3 manual interventions over a 0.3-mile campus route, demonstrating that simple sensor fusion with imitation learning can produce robust illumination-invariant driving policies.

Data Pipeline

The data pipeline synchronizes three heterogeneous sensor streams: an RGB camera at 30 Hz providing visual context, a thermal camera at 30 Hz capturing illumination-invariant features, and CAN bus telemetry at 20 Hz recording the expert driver's steering and throttle commands. All streams are temporally aligned and stored as synchronized training frames. The dataset captures diverse lighting conditions including direct sunlight, shade, dusk, and nighttime driving.

Multimodal Perception

The perception pipeline fuses RGB and thermal camera inputs into a joint representation that remains stable across lighting conditions. Where RGB features degrade at night, thermal features provide consistent road and obstacle signatures. The imitation learning model maps this fused representation directly to vehicle control commands, learning the expert's driving policy end-to-end without handcrafted rules.

Results

The system was evaluated on a 0.3-mile campus route under varying lighting conditions. The trained model achieved autonomous navigation with only 3 manual interventions, demonstrating reliable day-to-night generalization. This work was presented as a poster at the Southern California AI and Robotics Symposium (SCAIR 2025).
